Is AI Tilting the Cybersecurity Playing Field?

The AI Cyber Leverage Index (ACLI) tracks four empirical signals — CVE volume, severity distribution, exploitation velocity, and institutional discovery share — to assess whether AI is empowering defenders, attackers, or neither. Updated monthly from public vulnerability databases.

The Research Framework

Three Camps, Three Testable Predictions

The debate over AI's impact on cybersecurity maps onto three distinct schools of thought, each making falsifiable empirical predictions about the direction and magnitude of change. This index tracks which prediction is aging best as new data accumulates.

Accelerationist
AI favors defenders

Automated scanning, AI-assisted fuzzing, and LLM-powered triage tools find more vulnerabilities faster and shorten patch lag. Net result: a safer software ecosystem.

Safetyist
AI favors attackers

AI lowers the skill floor for exploit development, enables faster discovery of high-severity vulnerabilities, and outpaces the defensive side. The offense/defense gap widens.

Skeptic
AI is not the variable

CVE volume and severity have trended upward for decades. The marginal contribution of AI is overstated; structural factors — software complexity, disclosure norms — explain the trends.

Sub-metric 1 of 4

CVE Publication Volume

Monthly count of published CVEs from the NIST National Vulnerability Database, 1988–present. Rising volume is consistent with both AI-assisted discovery (Accelerationist) and AI-assisted offense (Safetyist). The Skeptic's prediction: no structural break around 2021–2023.

Sub-metric 2 of 4

Severity Distribution Shift (CVSS ≥ 7.0)

Rolling 12-month share of CVEs rated High or Critical (CVSS ≥ 7.0). An upward shift suggests AI is surfacing deeper, more exploitable vulnerabilities — which cuts both ways depending on who is finding them first.

A persistent upward trend post-2022 would be one of the strongest signals that AI is qualitatively changing what gets discovered, not merely increasing volume.

How to read this: The chart shows the percentage of all CVEs that month rated High or Critical. The red line is a 12-month rolling average to smooth out noise. A flat line means the share is stable; a rising line means an increasing fraction of newly discovered bugs are severe ones. Hover over any point to see the exact percentage for that month.

Sub-metric 3 of 4

Exploitation Velocity (CISA KEV)

Monthly new additions to CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog — the authoritative list of CVEs confirmed to be actively exploited in real-world attacks. Unlike CVE volume, this metric measures attacker behavior, not just disclosure.

The CISA KEV catalog launched in November 2021. Watching its monthly growth rate is the most direct empirical signal available on whether exploitation pace is accelerating in the AI era.

How to read this: Each red bar shows how many new CVEs were confirmed as actively exploited that month. The line is a 3-month average. Because this catalog only started in late 2021, the chart covers a shorter window than the others — but it is the most direct measure of attacker behavior available. Taller bars over time = faster exploitation. Note that the first month (November 2021) appears large because CISA added a backlog of pre-existing exploited vulnerabilities when launching the catalog.

Sub-metric 4 of 4 · Most Novel

Who Is Finding Vulnerabilities? (CNA Composition)

Share of CVEs attributed to large institutional actors — Google, Microsoft, GitHub Security Lab, Apple, Meta, Amazon — versus individual researchers and smaller vendors. Rising institutional share proxies for AI-assisted, at-scale discovery: large organizations are best-positioned to deploy AI scanning pipelines across entire codebases.

This is the most novel sub-metric in the ACLI framework. If institutional share rises sharply post-2022, it suggests AI is concentrating vulnerability discovery among the best-resourced defenders.

How to read this: The green shaded area shows what percentage of all CVEs in a given month were filed by large institutional actors (Google, Microsoft, Apple, etc.) rather than individual researchers or smaller vendors. A rising trend means big organizations are finding a larger share of all bugs — consistent with AI-assisted bulk scanning. The orange dashed lines again mark AI milestones. Look for whether the line bends upward after key releases.

Composite Index

The AI Cyber Leverage Index

The ACLI is a composite of the four sub-metrics above. Positive values indicate the current pattern is more consistent with the Safetyist prediction (attacker advantage); negative values favor the Accelerationist reading (defender advantage); near-zero is the Skeptic's preferred outcome.

Supplementary Signals

Additional Data Streams

Three independent datasets provide cross-validation and additional context beyond the four core sub-metrics. These signals are not included in the composite ACLI score (they cover shorter time horizons), but they offer converging or diverging evidence worth tracking.

Exploit-DB: Public Exploit Publications

How to read this: Each point is the number of new working exploits published publicly that month (left axis). The dashed line is the share of those exploits that cite a specific CVE (right axis). Rising volume means more offensive tools are freely available to any attacker. A rising CVE-linkage rate means exploits are increasingly tied to known, trackable vulnerabilities — a double-edged signal that helps defenders patch faster but also makes targeting trivial.

Observation
Accelerationist reads this as…

Rising CVE-linked exploit publication is actually a defender signal — tools like Metasploit and PoC code give red teams and patch teams the same information. Knowing which CVEs are exploitable drives faster institutional patching, not just more attacks.

Safetyist reads this as…

More public exploits lowers the skill floor for attackers dramatically. A working exploit that used to require a nation-state actor can now be run by a script kiddie within days of publication. AI-assisted code generation is accelerating this trend further.

Skeptic reads this as…

Exploit-DB volume has been roughly flat for years — there is no obvious structural break attributable to AI. The CVE-linkage trend reflects better disclosure hygiene, not a change in the underlying attacker/defender balance.

EPSS: Exploitation Probability Over Time

How to read this: EPSS (Exploit Prediction Scoring System) assigns each CVE a probability that it will be observed being exploited in the wild within the next 30 days. The solid line shows the monthly average EPSS score across all CVEs (left axis). The dashed line shows the percentage of CVEs scoring ≥ 0.10 — a meaningful risk threshold (right axis). EPSS data is available from May 2021 onwards.

Observation
Accelerationist reads this as…

If average EPSS is rising, it means a growing share of CVEs are being actively exploited — defenders are finding and fixing vulnerabilities faster than attackers can exploit them. Better detection infrastructure is translating into real-world exploitation prevention.

Safetyist reads this as…

An upward trend in high-risk EPSS scores means the attack surface is expanding faster than defenders can respond. AI-generated vulnerability research and automated exploit frameworks are converting theoretical CVEs into active threats at unprecedented speed.

Skeptic reads this as…

EPSS scores reflect attacker behavior that has been evolving for decades independent of AI. The model is trained on exploitation signals from security vendors — it may reflect improved detection and reporting more than a true change in attacker capability.

GitHub Security Advisories (GHSA)

How to read this: GitHub's Security Advisory database tracks vulnerabilities in open-source software supply chains — the libraries and packages that underpin most modern software. Each bar is the number of new advisories published in that month. A rising trend indicates either more vulnerabilities being discovered in open-source code, or improved disclosure infrastructure, or both.

Accelerationist reads this as…

More GHSA advisories means more eyes are on the supply chain — AI-assisted code review tools are finding latent vulnerabilities faster than attackers can exploit them, resulting in proactive disclosure before exploits exist in the wild.

Safetyist reads this as…

The explosion in supply-chain advisories is itself the attack surface expanding. Every open-source library is a potential entry point. AI lowers the bar for finding vulnerabilities in these libraries — but defenders cannot patch dependencies as fast as attackers can scan them.

Skeptic reads this as…

GHSA volume growth reflects the maturation of GitHub's disclosure program and broader CNA coverage — not an AI-driven structural change in the vulnerability landscape. Growth was already strong before 2022 and follows a secular trend driven by OSS adoption.

Methodology & Data Sources

How This Index Is Built

The ACLI updates automatically on the first of every month. A Python script queries five free public databases, processes the results, and saves simple JSON data files directly to this GitHub repository. The website reads those files client-side — no server required, no cost, fully transparent.

Transparency: All source data, processing scripts, and methodology are publicly available in the GitHub repository. Raw data lives in /data/. The fetching script is /scripts/fetch_data.py. Replace YOUR-USERNAME with your GitHub username once set up.

Score Calculation

Each sub-metric is z-scored relative to its pre-2022 baseline. The ACLI is the unweighted average of these four z-scores. A score between −0.5 and +0.5 is treated as ambiguous (Skeptic-consistent). Values above +0.5 support the Safetyist reading; below −0.5 support the Accelerationist reading.

Limitations: The ACLI cannot distinguish AI-driven from non-AI causes. It is a pattern-detection tool, not a causal model. The CNA attribution metric is particularly noisy. Treat the ACLI as a structured framework for tracking trends over time, not a definitive verdict.
